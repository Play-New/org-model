# Org-model agent — how it works

Generic by design: a manufacturer, a services firm, a platform, an AI company, a
brand, a non-profit — without assuming any of them.

## The lens

An org keeps promises to the outside world. A promise is never one-way — it's a
contract: the org gives something, gets something back.

**An org is the contracts it has with the world, plus what it leans on to keep
them.** Two things: contracts, and nodes.

The agent doesn't strategize or interpret. It maps the org against this lens, in
the org's own words. It can start knowing nothing, because the lens is only what's
true of every org.

This lens is the fixed, public **language** specified in `LANGUAGE.md` (built on
Cicero / Boundaryless + Promise Theory). The agent never changes the language to fit
the org — it is the **bridge** that re-expresses the org in it, citing every claim.
This doc is the operational view of that language; `LANGUAGE.md` is canonical.

## Contracts

One per outside party — mutual, usually unspoken. Each:

- **with** — who, outside (kept short — just the party)
- **org-gives** — what the org gives them (the promise it makes)
- **org-gets** — what comes back that keeps it alive
- **constraints** — what has to hold, or the contract breaks
- **measures** — how you know it's actually happening, both ways (observed, not targets)

Healthy / strained / broken is read off measures and constraints, not declared.

Each contract carries a **detailed prose note**, written like a short legal
document, in five `##` sections: **The parties** (who they are, with specifics) ·
**What &lt;org&gt; gives** · **What &lt;org&gt; gets back** · **The terms** (the
constraints) · **How we know** (the measures, with the actual figures). Inline
`(source-id)` citations throughout. Rich documents → rich note.

## Nodes

What the contracts rest on — people, money, tools, a brand, a relationship, a
method. Grouped into ~10-12, not every task. Each:

- **role** — core (delivers a contract), service (serves the core), or platform
  (keeps the org standing: legal, HR, finance…)
- **keeps** — which contracts it holds up
- **made of** — what it consists of, key people included
- **needs now** — what it takes to stand today (can point to other nodes)

## What the map shows on its own

- which resource the org really runs on — often not what they think
- where it rests on one irreplaceable thing: a person, a supplier, a mechanism
- core vs platform — how much exists just to keep the org running
- where the promise is louder than the delivery
- who gets the value vs who pays — often not the same

## The five steps — documents first, agent leads

Open light: *"in two lines, what do you do and for whom?"* Then:

The agent works **one stage at a time**: it does not start the nodes until the
contracts are complete and the user has confirmed them. Pacing is about how *many*
items per turn, never how *deep* — every contract and node is detailed.

1. **Contracts** — find them all, wide before deep. Every outward-facing surface is
   one. Docs: site, statute, a deck, a client contract (the gives side); the budget
   (the gets side). Finish and confirm contracts before any node.
2. **Nodes** — take the org chart, remap it onto the contracts, sort core / service
   / platform. (The org chart is how they're arranged; the contracts are what
   matters. Map the first onto the second, not the reverse.)
3. **Decompose** — for each load-bearing node: what it's made of, what it needs now.
4. **Check** — every contract has a core node? every unit placed? anything keeping no
   contract = overhead. A contract with nothing coming back = how is it funded?
5. **Identity** — say what the org is, from the structure. Where that clashes with how
   they described it, that's the finding.

## Rules

- Documents first — they don't flatter. Questions only fill the gaps.
- As-is, never should-be. Measures are observed, not targets.
- Answers become cited sources.
- Doc vs word contradiction → flag it, don't resolve it quietly.
- The agent leads; the person supplies and confirms; nothing is written without an ok.

Later, live data feeds the measures and keeps the map current — built first with a
person, then kept alive by data.
